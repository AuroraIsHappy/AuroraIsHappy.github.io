Revisiting Transformer Basics (and rethinking what we do for agents in 2026 from a transformer vs. brain perspective)

Posted on 2026-06-23 In learning-notes Word count in article: 17k Reading time ≈ 16 mins.

Background:

I’ve been exploring papers on LLM agents (memory, agentic RL, multi-agent systems) quite a lot for the past two months. I came across lots of effective methods, but I felt confused when asking myself, “How should I view these methods from a relatively unified perspective?” and “What deserves trying next?” I also felt that although “thinking from your agent’s perspective” is a great design strategy, perhaps I shouldn’t view models as humans all the time because the architectures of transformers and the human brain are completely different. That’s why I decided to step back and review the internals of the transformer (code & math) to remind myself what is happening under the hood.

I also recorded a few recent immature thoughts on transformers vs. brains, and my rethinking about what we have done for agents in 2026 (from super-human knowledge to human-like “intelligence”). I hope I’m not making too many mistakes in this part.

Transformer Basics

Input Embedding

  • NLP: Word -> Vector
  • Speech: Frame -> Vector
  • Image: Pixels/Patches -> Vector
  • Graph: Node & Edge -> Vector (a molecule can also be modeled as a graph)

Positional Encoding

  1. Sinusoidal position representations (original paper)

Pros:

  • Periodicity indicates that maybe “absolute position” isn’t as important
  • Maybe can extrapolate to longer sequences as periods restart

Cons:

  • Not learnable; also the extrapolation doesn’t really work
  1. Learned absolute position representations
  1. Other (RoPE, etc.)

Then these two vectors are added during the forward pass.

Encoder

The encoder is composed of a stack of N identical layers (they do not share weights).

Multi-head attention

X (input matrix) * W_i_q,W_i_k,W_i_v (weight matrices) -> Q_i,K_i,V_i (attention matrices) -> multiply & softmax -> different Z matrices -> concat -> * a weight matrix W_o -> the output Z matrix (with enough information from the sequence) to be sent forward to the FFN

Feed Forward

MLP

Additional:

  • Residual connections
    $x_\ell = F(x_{\ell-1}) + x_{\ell-1}$

Deep networks are bad at learning identity functions. Adding the input to the output prevents the network from “forgetting” or distorting important information as it is processed by many layers.

  • Layer Normalization

$$
x^{\ell’} = \frac{x^{\ell} - \mu^{\ell}}{\sigma^{\ell} + \epsilon}
$$

Incorporate learned weight and bias:

  • weight decay $\theta_t \leftarrow(1-\beta) \theta_{t-1}-\alpha g_t$

Decoder

The decoder is also composed of a stack of N identical layers.

Masked Self-Attention

  • mask information about future tokens to -inf

  • output = softmax $\left(Q K^{T} / \sqrt{d_{k}}\right) V$

Cross attention

k & v from encoder, q from the decoder

generate a probability distribution

Advantage: Rotate the vector according to its position, preserving the angles and the dot products between these vectors.

AlphaFold

AlphaFold is regarded by many people as “the most useful thing AI has ever done”. I am not familiar with non-NLP areas and was surprised to learn that AlphaFold is based on an architecture quite similar to a transformer, if we view the system as three parts (featurization, encoder, and decoder).

I had intended to record some algorithm details here, but later found myself writing down almost everything in Supplementary Information § 1.6-1.8 because each additional block is so exquisite. Information (features & relations) is modeled into tensors & operations, and each kind of information is incorporated into the computational process recursively.

I kind of understand why “language is blessed”, as there are many fewer relationships to attend to, so relatively simple transformer architectures work quite well. Information makes up the world, but atomic information without sufficient “relations” is meaningless and misleading. I felt that maybe the power of “attention” is still underexplored if we compare commonly used mechanisms with those in this awesome protein-predicting system.

Human Brains

Wang et al. (2026) diagnosed seven typical agent failure modes in their cross-domain study: Instruction Failure, Environmental Failure, False Assumptions, Planning Error, Memory Limitations, Catastrophic Forgetting (key information remains in context but is omitted), and History Error Accumulation (a single early error silently cascades into a failed task). I can’t help thinking that these can be easily avoided by a careful human.

I was curious about how human brains avoid such mistakes. We humans are quite good at realizing when we are wrong, either from external feedback or internal feelings. We are also blessed with the ability for continual learning and multi-level memory accumulation thanks to our brains. All these abilities are quite generalizable across tasks, with no need for RL training in specific environments. I read a few essays and found the underlying mechanisms quite interesting and inspiring.

One-shot perceptual learning (Hachisuka et al., 2026)

Human one-shot perceptual learning refers to the remarkable ability to change perception after a single exposure to a disambiguating experience. In this study, it is defined as a phenomenon “whereby a single viewing experience drastically alters visual perception in a long-lasting manner”.
A typical example is the Mooney image paradigm: initially ambiguous images become easily recognizable after a single exposure to their clear counterparts, and this improvement can persist for months.

The core neural mechanism proposed in this paper is that the learned “prior” is stored in the high-level visual cortex (HLVC), where neural plasticity supports one-shot perceptual learning, rather than in the hippocampus or distributed higher-order control networks. A prior is a structured template that can directly act on visual processing, not knowledge at the linguistic or conceptual level.

In this study, researchers also implemented a transformer model that captures human behavior (with prior storage module, top-down conditioning) during the one-shot perceptual learning task (a clear image & an ambiguous image).

Just-In-Time Reinforcement Learning: Continual Learning in LLM Agents Without Gradient Updates (Li et al., 2026)

This reminded me of a recent inspiring paper on agentic RL (Li et al., 2026). They didn’t do gradient updates and instead directly modulate the LLM’s output logits (by retrieving relevant trajectories to estimate action advantages). The rule is as follows:

$$
z^{\prime}(s,a) = z(s,a) + \beta \cdot \hat{A}(s,a)
$$

This method avoids the enormous computational costs and catastrophic forgetting caused by conventional RL, while preserving the generality of RL compared with In-Context Learning (explicit textual descriptions fail to realize things or skills that are better felt than said).

They name their algorithm “JitRL (Just-In-Time Reinforcement Learning)” and theoretically prove that this additive update rule is the exact closed-form solution to the KL-constrained policy optimization objective. Maybe such RL methods are closer to the flexibility of our brains’ learning process…

Error monitoring

Human error processing is a system for detecting when an action outcome is worse than expected. This involves:

  • Internal Error Monitoring
  • External Feedback

ACC/dACC (dorsal anterior cingulate cortex) acts as a central monitoring region for both processes (Holroyd et al., 2004).

Flow:

1. The brain forms an intended action
2. The brain compares action with intention
  • If a mismatch is detected internally, an early error signal appears (Gehring et al., 1993)
3. Internal error detection produces ERN (error-related negativity)
  • ERN reflects internal self-monitoring: the brain quickly detects that something went wrong before external feedback is needed (peaks in ~50ms after action), and before the person consciously realizes it (Nieuwenhuis et al., 2001).
  • It is worth noting that ERN reflects the operation of an abstract system for error detection and is shared by many unrelated tasks, as suggested in Dehaene’s review (2018).
4. External feedback produces FRN (feedback-related negativity) only when it adds new information after ERN (Heldmann et al., 2008)
5. The error signal supports behavioral adjustment

Our brain is doing RL without our consciousness almost every minute.

Retuning of hippocampal representations during sleep (Maboudi et al., 2024)

The dreaming mechanism of Claude Code (Anthropic, 2026) has become popular recently, but I have been wondering whether dreaming is limited to information abundance and experience summarization (such as learning or training beyond memorized facts). I personally feel that ~4 hours of sleep keeps me from mastering much of the knowledge from the previous day. The study by Maboudi et al. (2024) revealed the following mechanisms in mice navigating a maze:

  • sleep representations involve reorganization and retuning, not faithful replay
  • modifications of spatial representations would take place during sleep

    The similarity of sleep representations to maze PFs decreased progressively.

  • the ripple representations during sleep predicted the future place fields of neurons during re-exposure to the environment
  • hippocampal activity supports generative simulation

    Trajectories simulated by the hippocampus during sleep ripples may explore pathways that were not directly experienced during waking.

So the dreaming mechanism of the brain seems to have an extra component of retuning, predicting, and trying. Is this mechanism partly similar to world models? I’m not sure, but I will come back to this blog when I learn more about it.

Reflection

Agent systems are systems that are able to accomplish goals on their own, make active decisions, and make plans… You have to have a system that can actively solve problems for you to get to AGI… Agents are that path… —— Demis Hassabis

LLMs are moving from chatboxes to agents that can complete work for us. 2026 has witnessed an outbreak in agent research. From agentic RL, harness, to self-evolving methods (context-level, harness-level, parameter-level), all efforts seem to be pushing models from super-human knowledge (gained by large-scale pre-training and transformer architecture) toward human-like intelligence (which needs extra structure and information flow).

Maybe the argument in A Brief History of Intelligence is an inspiring reminder of what intelligence (instead of knowledge) is.

Can we view the recent agent-related methods as ways to make these abilities more explicit?

  • Harness (narrow definition) → steering (#1)
  • Agentic RL → reinforcement (#2), simulating (#3) (the ones with critic)
  • Extra planning/reflection mechanisms & long reasoning process → simulating (#3)
  • Self-evolving → reinforcement (#2), simulating (#3)
  • Multi-agent collaboration → mentalizing (#4), speaking (#5)

…
